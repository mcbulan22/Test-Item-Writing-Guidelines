Part 1  ·  Guidelines in Writing Different Types of Questions

The following guidelines provide a comprehensive, step-by-step framework for crafting each type of assessment item. Each section includes a structured process, design principles, worked examples, a design checklist, and a Do's and Don'ts summary.

1. Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)

Assess recall, comprehension, application, analysis, and evaluation

General Strategy Stem Design Distractor Design Examples Do's & Don'ts
Multiple choice questions are among the most versatile assessment tools available. When well-constructed, they assess a broad range of cognitive skills — from factual recall through comprehension and application, up to higher-order analysis and evaluation. They can also serve as powerful pre-assessment tools and their distractors anchor rich post-test discussions. However, MCQs are difficult to construct well — poorly written items reward guessing and measure test-taking skill rather than genuine competence.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define Objective
Identify the specific knowledge or functional competence the question aims to assess. Anchor every item to a stated learning outcome before writing a single word.
Map each item to one outcome. If you cannot name the outcome it measures, revise or discard the item.
2
Draft the Stem
Write a clear, complete, direct question presenting one defined problem. A student who knows the material should be able to answer it without looking at the options. Avoid negatives unless essential; when used, capitalize the negative word (e.g., NOT).
Cover the options and read only the stem. If the question is unclear without the choices, rewrite it.
3
Construct Options
Provide 4 options: one correct answer and 3 plausible distractors. Build distractors from real student misconceptions or common errors. Ensure grammatical consistency across all options and arrange them in logical order.
After drafting, scan for repeated opening words across options — move them into the stem.
4
Review Content Validity
Confirm the correct answer is the single, clearly defensible best response. Distractors should reflect partial truths or common mistakes. Avoid "all of the above," "none of the above," and absolute terms (always, never).
Ask a colleague to identify the correct answer cold. If they hesitate between two options, revise.
5
Peer Review
Have another assessor review for ambiguity, fairness, bias, and alignment with the learning outcome. Verify that no unintentional cues (length, grammar, absolute words) signal the answer.
6
Record and Validate
Record the item in the Test Item Bank with: the learning outcome, answer key, cognitive level (Bloom's), and distractor rationale. Tag items for future reuse and post-test analysis.

Key Design Principles

A
Align with Learning Outcomes
Every question must trace directly to a stated learning outcome. A technically correct item from a test bank is not automatically a good fit — always evaluate borrowed items before use.
Write a few items each week throughout the term rather than all at the end.
B
Use Familiar Language
Write in language all students understand. Do not introduce new terminology within a question or use culturally specific expressions that may disadvantage some learners.
C
Instruct Students to Select the Best Answer
Make it clear before the test that students choose the single best response. Distractors may contain a partial element of truth — that is intentional and by design.
D
Avoid Trick Questions
MCQs should reward genuine knowledge, not catch students on subtle wording traps. A student who has mastered the material should identify the correct answer without second-guessing ambiguous phrasing.
E
Randomly Distribute Correct Responses
Ensure correct answers are distributed roughly evenly across positions (a, b, c, d) across the full test. Predictable patterns enable guessing and undermine validity.
After drafting all items, tally answer key positions and redistribute if one position dominates.
F
Use the Test as a Learning Opportunity
A post-test discussion of why each distractor was wrong — and why the correct answer is right — reinforces learning and reveals student misconceptions.

Stem Design — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR STEM
Which of these assessment findings indicates that the client does NOT need to be suctioned?
a) Diminished breath sounds
b) Absence of adventitious breath sounds
c) Inability to cough up sputum
d) Wheezing following bronchodilator therapy
Issues: Negative wording ("does NOT"), options needed to understand the question, unclear intent, filler phrasing.
✓ IMPROVED STEM
A nurse assesses a client with pneumonia. Which finding most clearly indicates the client requires suctioning?
a) Absence of adventitious breath sounds
b) Respiratory rate of 18 breaths per minute
c) Inability to cough up secretions
d) Wheezing prior to bronchodilator therapy
Improved: Positive framing, full context in stem, direct question, one clear answer (c).

Distractor Design — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR DISTRACTORS
How long does a biennial plant generally live?
a) It dies after the second year
b) It lives for many years
c) It lives for more than one year
d) It needs to be replanted every two years
Issues: Options overlap (c includes b and d), non-mutually exclusive, inconsistent format, multiple defensible answers.
✓ IMPROVED DISTRACTORS
How long does a biennial plant generally live?
a) One year
b) Two years
c) Several years
d) More than ten years
Improved: Mutually exclusive, consistent phrasing, logical numerical order, one clear answer (b).

✓State the full problem in the stem
✓Use direct questions, not incomplete sentences
✓Capitalize or underline negatives (NOT) when unavoidable
✓Build distractors from real student misconceptions

✗Include "All of the above" or "None of the above"
✗Open stems with "Which of the following…" filler

✗Use overlapping or non-mutually-exclusive options
✗Write far-fetched distractors that are obviously wrong
✗Include absolute terms: always, never, all, none, only
✗Make the correct answer visibly longer than distractors
✗Introduce new or unfamiliar terminology in the item
✗Use test bank items without checking outcome alignment

2. Essay Questions

Assess analysis, synthesis, evaluation, and extended argumentation

Higher-Order Thinking Rubric Design Prompt Writing Examples Do's & Don'ts
Essay questions are the primary tool for assessing higher-order thinking. When well-designed, they require students to organize ideas, construct arguments, apply knowledge to problems, and communicate reasoning clearly. The challenge lies in writing prompts specific enough to focus responses, while open enough to elicit genuine thinking — and in developing rubrics that make scoring consistent and defensible.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define Purpose and Cognitive Level
Identify which higher-order competence the question will measure: analysis, synthesis, or evaluation. Use Bloom's Taxonomy to confirm the intended level before writing.
Ask: "What do I want students to do — describe it, apply it, critique it, or create with it?" Let the answer drive the verb you choose.
2
Write the Prompt with Directive Verbs
Open the prompt with a specific directive verb: Analyze, Compare, Evaluate, Justify, Synthesize, Critique, Propose. Provide enough context for students to understand the problem without leading them toward a particular answer.
Avoid vague prompts like "Discuss X." Instead: "Evaluate the effectiveness of X in achieving Y, and justify your position with evidence."
3
Clarify Parameters
Explicitly state the expected scope, depth, length, and time. Ambiguity in parameters produces wildly variable responses and makes fair scoring nearly impossible. Example: "Write a 300–400 word response. You have 25 minutes."
If a word count is specified, state what counts toward it. Clarity here prevents disputes during marking.
4
Develop the Scoring Rubric
Create a rubric before finalizing the prompt. A good rubric covers: content accuracy and depth, quality of reasoning and argument, use of evidence, organization and coherence, and clarity of expression. Each criterion should have at least 3 performance levels with descriptors.
If you find the rubric hard to write, the prompt is probably too vague. Revise the prompt until the rubric writes itself.
5
Review for Clarity and Bias
Read the prompt from a student's perspective: is the task unambiguous? Could any student — regardless of background — engage with it on equal footing? Have a colleague attempt to draft a quick response to test depth.
6
Validate and Record
Include the approved version in the Assessment Instrument Register with the rubric attached, the learning outcome mapped, the cognitive level tagged, and the estimated response time noted.

Essay Prompt — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR PROMPT
Discuss climate change.
(No constraints, no directive, no scope)
Issues: No directive verb, no scope, no length or time parameters, no clear cognitive demand. Students cannot know what depth or angle to take.
✓ IMPROVED PROMPT
Evaluate two proposed policy responses to climate change. Argue which approach is more likely to achieve long-term emissions reduction, using evidence from course materials.
(300–400 words · 25 minutes)
Improved: Directive verb (Evaluate/Argue), clear scope (two policies), evidence requirement, length and time specified.

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid

ScopeSpecify exactly what content to addressOverly broad prompts that allow any response

Essay Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓Use specific directive verbs (Analyze, Evaluate, Justify)
✓Provide enough context for a focused, targeted response
✓State expected length, depth, and time clearly
✓Develop the rubric before finalizing the prompt
✓Include criteria for reasoning, evidence, and organization
✓Have a colleague attempt the prompt before issuing it
✓Attach the rubric to the recorded item in the register
✗ DON'T
✗Use "Discuss…" without specifying what to discuss
✗Leave scope, length, or time parameters unstated
✗Score responses without a rubric (impressionistic marking)
✗Set prompts that only require recall or summarization
✗Use topics that systematically disadvantage some students
✗Finalize a prompt you cannot write a rubric for
✗Omit the rubric from the Assessment Instrument Register

3. Fill-in-the-Blank / Completion Questions

Assess factual recall, precise terminology, and specific knowledge

Recall Assessment Blank Placement Answer Key Design Examples Do's & Don'ts
Fill-in-the-blank items are efficient tools for assessing precise recall of facts, terminology, formulas, and specific concepts. Because students must produce the answer rather than recognize it, these items are more demanding than true/false or matching and less susceptible to guessing. The key challenge is writing statements that have one — and only one — clearly correct answer, without unintentionally giving away the answer through grammar or context.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Identify Target Knowledge
Select only facts, terms, formulas, or concepts that are central to the learning outcome and worth students recalling precisely. Avoid trivial details mentioned only in passing.
Ask: "Is precise recall of this specific term a meaningful indicator of competence?" If not, choose a different item type.
2
Construct the Statement
Write a complete, meaningful sentence with one or two blanks placed at or near the end — not at the beginning. Blanks at the start require students to hold the whole sentence in memory. Limit to one blank per sentence; two blanks dramatically increase ambiguity.
Read the statement aloud without the answer. If it sounds natural with an obvious gap, it is well-written.
3
Avoid Grammatical Giveaways
Articles (a vs. an), verb agreement, and plural forms can unintentionally signal the correct answer. Review each statement to ensure grammar does not narrow the possible answers.
Replace a/an before a blank with "a/an (as appropriate)" or restructure the sentence to remove the clue.
4
Define the Expected Answer Precisely
Before finalizing, write out every acceptable correct answer — including synonyms, abbreviations, alternate spellings, and equivalent forms. If the list becomes too long, the item is too ambiguous and should be revised.
If you find yourself accepting three or more substantially different answers, the blank probably doesn't narrow the answer enough.
5
Review Readability and Context
Ensure the remaining context clearly points to the missing information. If a student who knows the content might still be unsure what type of answer is expected, add a cue in parentheses, e.g., "(in years)" or "(specific term)".
6
Record in Item Bank
Store in the Item Bank with: the complete answer key (including all acceptable variations), the learning outcome, and competence type tagged as "Knowledge-based." Note any acceptable abbreviations or alternative phrasings.

Fill-in-the-Blank — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR ITEM
__________ discovered penicillin.
(Blank at beginning; lacks context; grammar ambiguous)
Issues: Blank at start, lacks context, grammatically ambiguous, could accept many names or phrases.
✓ IMPROVED ITEM
Penicillin was discovered in 1928 by __________ (scientist's surname).
(Blank at end; context provided; parenthetical cue added)
Improved: Blank at end, meaningful context, parenthetical cue narrows expected answer format. Answer: Fleming.

GrammarNeutral articles; no grammatical cluesa/an or verb form revealing the answer type

Fill-in-the-Blank Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓Place blanks at or near the end of sentences
✓Use one blank per sentence where possible
✓Write all acceptable answer variations before finalizing
✓Add a parenthetical cue when the answer type is unclear
✓Test only central, meaningful knowledge
✓Review grammar to remove unintentional answer clues
✓Store item with full answer key and all acceptable variants
✗ DON'T
✗Place blanks at the beginning of sentences
✗Include two or more blanks in a single sentence
✗Use a/an immediately before a blank
✗Accept so many variants that the item becomes unclear
✗Test trivial or incidental details
✗Allow grammar to signal whether the answer is singular/plural
✗Finalize without checking for grammatical giveaways

4. Identification / Short Answer Questions

Assess understanding, reasoning, and focused recall in the student's own words

Focused Recall Answer Key Design Format Clarity Examples Do's & Don'ts
Short answer and identification questions occupy a productive middle ground — more cognitively demanding than MCQ (no provided choices) while more focused and scorable than full essays. They require students to produce a response in their own words: a phrase, a definition, a brief explanation. The key to success is precision: in wording, in format expectations, and in the answer key.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define the Competence Level
Identify whether the item targets recall (name, list, define), comprehension (explain, describe, summarize), or application/analysis (apply, distinguish, explain why). The level determines the verb and scoring criteria.
Match your verb to Bloom's: Define/List/Name (Knowledge) → Explain/Describe (Comprehension) → Apply/Distinguish (Higher order).
2
Draft the Question Prompt
Use precise, direct directive verbs: Define, List, Name, Identify, Explain, Describe, State, Calculate. Avoid vague verbs like "discuss" or "talk about." The prompt should make the expected task unambiguous.
Write your own model answer first. If you find yourself uncertain what to include, the prompt needs to be more specific.
3
Specify the Expected Response Format
Explicitly state how much to write: "in one sentence," "in 2–3 sentences," "list three items," "in no more than 50 words." Without format guidance, responses range wildly, making consistent scoring nearly impossible.
Include format guidance in the question itself, not just in general instructions at the top of the test.
4
Develop a Detailed Answer Key
Write out a full model response before finalizing the item. Identify: the minimum acceptable response, acceptable alternate phrasings, and what partial credit looks like (if applicable). The key should be detailed enough for any marker to apply consistently.
If two markers applying the same key would award substantially different marks, the key needs more detail.
5
Review for Accuracy, Fairness, and Cognitive Demand
Confirm the item is factually accurate, unambiguous, and fair to all students. Check that it genuinely requires the cognitive level intended — not just recall in disguise. Have a peer review before finalizing.
6
Record and Archive
Include the final version in the Item Bank with: the model answer, acceptable variations, partial credit criteria (if used), learning outcome, and cognitive level. This supports consistent re-use and moderation.

Short Answer — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR ITEM
Talk about photosynthesis.
(No format, no scope, no directive verb)
Issues: "Talk about" is too vague — students cannot know if they should define, explain, or evaluate. No format or length guidance given.
✓ IMPROVED ITEM
Explain the two main stages of photosynthesis and identify where in the plant cell each stage occurs. (2–4 sentences)
(Clear directive verb, specific scope, format stated)
Improved: Directive verb (Explain/Identify), specific scope (two stages + location), format stated (2–4 sentences).

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid
Directive VerbPrecise: Define, List, Explain, IdentifyVague: "talk about," "write about," "discuss"
ScopeSpecify exactly what content to addressOpen-ended scope that allows any response
FormatState required length or format explicitlyLeaving response length or format unstated
Answer KeyWrite model answer before finalizing the itemVague key that markers apply inconsistently
Cognitive LevelConfirm the item demands the intended levelRecall questions disguised as higher-order items
AlignmentMaps to a specific learning outcomeItems that test content never covered in class

Short Answer Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓Use precise directive verbs (Define, List, Explain, Identify)
✓Specify the expected format and length in the question
✓Write a model answer before finalizing the item
✓Develop an answer key detailed enough for any marker
✓Include partial credit criteria if partial marks will be awarded
✓Confirm the item targets the intended cognitive level
✓Archive with model answer and competence level tagged
✗ DON'T
✗Use vague verbs like "discuss" or "write about"
✗Leave response length or format unspecified
✗Finalize before writing the model answer
✗Allow markers to interpret the key subjectively
✗Write prompts that only require memorized recall when higher order is intended
✗Test content that was not meaningfully taught or practiced
✗Store without a detailed answer key and outcome mapping

5. True or False Questions

Assess essential factual knowledge and conceptual understanding quickly

Single-Idea Focus Absolute Accuracy Language Review Examples Do's & Don'ts
True/false items are the most efficient format for assessing large amounts of factual content quickly. Their primary weakness is a 50% guessing probability, so they should be used in clusters of 10 or more and always paired with a rationale requirement when higher cognitive demand is desired. The defining challenge is writing statements that are unambiguously and entirely true or entirely false.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Identify the Learning Point
Focus on a single, important concept, fact, or principle students should know precisely. True/false items are not suited to nuanced topics where context changes the answer. Reserve them for clear-cut, well-established content.
Ask: "Is this statement unambiguously true or false regardless of context?" If it depends on assumptions, choose a different item type.
2
Construct the Statement
Write a single concise, factual sentence in clear, plain language. Each statement should contain exactly one idea — never combine two separate facts, as a student may agree with one and not the other, creating genuine ambiguity.
Read the statement to someone unfamiliar with the content. If they ask a clarifying question, revise the statement.
3
Ensure the Statement Is 100% True or 100% False
Partial truths are the most common flaw in true/false items. A statement that is "mostly true" or "true under certain conditions" is not acceptable. If you find yourself writing qualifications or exceptions, the content is too nuanced for this format.
Have a subject matter expert review the statement. If they hesitate or add "well, it depends," revise the item.
4
Avoid Negatives and Double Negatives
Negative constructions slow reading, increase confusion, and often produce unintended ambiguity. Avoid "not," "except," and especially double negatives. If a negative is genuinely necessary, bold or underline the negative word.
5
Avoid Absolute Qualifiers and Multi-Idea Statements
Words like always, never, all, only, and none make statements almost always false — students learn to exploit this. Steer toward direct factual claims without universal qualifiers.
6
Validate, Approve, and Record
Peer review for factual accuracy, clarity, and absence of language bias. Use true and false items in roughly equal proportions to prevent pattern-guessing. Record in the Item Bank with the answer key and competence dimension (typically Knowledge).
Aim for a roughly equal split of true and false items across the full set to reduce guessing strategies.

True/False — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR STATEMENT
Plants always absorb water through their roots and never obtain it from any other source.
True  /  False
Issues: Two absolute claims ("always," "never"), combines two ideas, entirely false due to overstatement. Students can guess False without knowing the content.
✓ IMPROVED STATEMENT
The majority of a land plant's water uptake occurs through the root system.
True  /  False   (Answer: True)
Improved: Single idea, qualified language ("majority"), factually accurate as True, cannot be guessed from absolute qualifiers.

✓Focus each statement on one single, clear idea
✓Ensure the statement is unambiguously 100% true or false
✓Use plain, direct language without negatives
✓Balance true and false items roughly equally across the set
✓Target essential, meaningful knowledge (not trivial details)
✓Have a subject matter expert verify factual accuracy
✓Use in clusters of 10+ for adequate reliability
✗ DON'T
✗Combine two ideas or facts in a single statement
✗Write statements that are only partially true
✗Use double negatives or "not" constructions
✗Use absolute terms: always, never, all, none, only
✗Test obscure details barely mentioned in instruction
✗Allow a heavy imbalance of true vs. false items
✗Rely on true/false alone for high-stakes summative assessment

6. Matching Questions

Assess ability to recognize relationships between concepts, terms, and definitions

Relationship Testing List Construction Layout Design Examples Do's & Don'ts
Matching items efficiently test whether students can identify correct relationships between two sets of related content — such as terms and definitions, causes and effects, or concepts and examples. They are space-efficient, reduce guessing better than true/false, and are easy to administer. The key design challenges are ensuring all items in each column are homogeneous, that correct matches are unambiguous, and that dummy responses prevent elimination by process of elimination.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define the Relationship Type
Clearly identify what type of relationship students will match: term ↔ definition, cause ↔ effect, event ↔ date, concept ↔ example. The relationship type must be consistent across all pairs — do not mix different relationship types in one exercise.
State the relationship explicitly in the instructions: "Match each term in Column A with its correct definition in Column B."
2
Prepare Homogeneous, Parallel Lists
Develop 6–10 pairs. All items in Column A (stems) should belong to the same category, and all items in Column B (responses) should belong to the same category. Mixing categories makes the correct matches too obvious.
Read all Column A items together — they should feel like members of the same set. Do the same for Column B. If they don't, revise for homogeneity.
3
Add Dummy Responses
Include 1–2 extra responses in Column B that have no correct match in Column A. This prevents students from using process of elimination for the final answer without actually knowing it. Dummy responses should be as plausible as the correct responses.
4
Write Clear, Unambiguous Instructions
State explicitly: (a) what relationship to match, (b) whether each response can be used more than once or only once, and (c) how to record the answer. Ambiguous instructions are a common source of avoidable student error.
5
Organize the Layout Thoughtfully
Place longer text (definitions, descriptions) in Column A (left) and shorter text (terms, names, dates) in Column B (right). Arrange Column B responses in logical order — alphabetical, numerical, or chronological — to speed up scanning.
6
Review for Plausibility and Validate
Ensure that no unintended clues link a stem to its response (grammar, repeated keywords, length matching). All responses should be plausible for at least some stems. Submit the finalized set to the Item Bank for inclusion in the approved question pool.
Swap the columns and re-read. If any match becomes obvious from surface features rather than knowledge, revise.

Matching — Poor vs. Improved Example

✗ POOR MATCHING SET
Column A: 1. Mitosis   2. Napoleon   3. H₂O   4. Renaissance
Column B: A. Water   B. French general   C. Cell division   D. Art movement
Issues: Mixed categories (biology, history, chemistry, art history) make correct matches obvious without content knowledge.
✓ IMPROVED MATCHING SET
Column A (Cell Processes): 1. Mitosis   2. Meiosis   3. Apoptosis   4. Cytokinesis
Column B (Definitions): A. Division of cytoplasm   B. Programmed cell death   C. Produces genetically identical cells   D. Produces gametes   E. ★ Protein synthesis (dummy)
Improved: Homogeneous category, parallel definitions, one dummy response (E) prevents elimination guessing.

Part 2  ·  Guidelines for Selecting Assessment Item Types

Choosing the appropriate assessment item type is as important as writing the item well. The decision should be guided by the cognitive level being assessed, the nature of the content, logistical constraints, and the overall purpose of the assessment. Best practice is to use a balanced mix of item types — guided by a test blueprint — to ensure both validity and reliability.

Key Factors in Selecting Item Types

1
Learning Objective / Competency Level (Bloom's Taxonomy)
The most important factor. Match the item type to the cognitive level being assessed:
Knowledge / Recall — True/False, Fill-in-the-Blank, Matching
Comprehension / Understanding — MCQ, Short Answer
Application — Scenario-based MCQ, Short Answer
Analysis / Evaluation / Synthesis — Essay, Extended Response, Case Studies
2
Nature of the Content
Align the item format with what the content requires learners to do:
Factual, discrete content (dates, definitions, terms) — Matching, Fill-in-the-Blank, True/False
Conceptual understanding — MCQ
Relationships between ideas — Matching
Complex reasoning or argumentation — Essay
3
Number of Examinees / Scoring Efficiency
Consider the scale of administration and available marking resources:
Large groups — Objective items (MCQ, T/F, Matching) for fast, consistent scoring
Small groups or individual — Subjective items (Essay, Short Answer) are more feasible
4
Time Constraints
Available time affects how many and which types of items to include:
Limited time — Objective items allow broader content coverage per minute
Sufficient time — Essay and performance tasks enable deeper, more authentic assessment
5
Reliability vs. Validity Trade-off
Every assessment design involves balancing these two measurement properties:
Objective items — Higher reliability (consistent scoring), but may sacrifice construct validity for complex skills
Subjective items — Higher validity for real-world competencies, but require rubrics to ensure reliability
6
Purpose of Assessment
The intended use of results should shape item selection:
Formative (ongoing checks) — Short Answer, T/F, Fill-in-the-Blank
Summative (end-of-unit/course) — Mixed item sets (MCQ + Essay) or performance tasks
Diagnostic — MCQ with targeted distractors, Short Answer
7
Mixing Item Types (Test Blueprints)
Best practice is to use a combination of item types in a single assessment to:
Cover different cognitive levels across Bloom's Taxonomy
Accommodate different learner strengths and expression styles
Improve content validity by representing the full curriculum scope
Balance scoring efficiency with depth of measurement

Quick Reference — When to Use Each Item Type

MCQ
Broad coverage · Efficient scoring · All cognitive levels
Essay
Higher-order thinking · Argumentation · Synthesis
Fill-in-Blank
Factual recall · Precise terminology · Knowledge
Short Answer
Focused recall · Reasoning in own words · Flexibility
True / False
Quick screening · Essential facts · Knowledge check
Matching
Relationships · Terminology pairs · Recall efficiency
Best Practice Reminder: No single item type can measure all learning outcomes. Design every assessment using a test blueprint, align items to outcomes, use a mix of types to ensure validity and reliability, and store all finalized items in the institutional Test Item Bank or Assessment Instrument Register with a review date attached.
