Guidelines in Writing Different Types of Questions

Each section includes a step-by-step process, design principles, worked examples, a checklist, and Do's & Don'ts

1
Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ)
Assess recall, comprehension, application, analysis, and evaluation
General StrategyStem DesignDistractor DesignMarine Examples
▼
Multiple choice questions are among the most versatile assessment tools in maritime education. When well-constructed, they assess a broad range of cognitive skills — from recall of SOLAS regulations and engine components, through comprehension of watch-keeping duties, up to application of ColRegs in navigational scenarios. Poorly written items reward guessing and measure test-taking skill rather than genuine maritime competence.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define Objective
Identify the specific maritime knowledge or competency the question aims to assess — e.g., STCW watch-keeping standards, ColRegs light configurations, engine room procedures, or MARPOL discharge restrictions. Anchor every item to a stated learning outcome.
If you cannot name the specific maritime competency the item measures, revise or discard it before writing options.
2
Draft the Stem
Write a clear, complete, direct question presenting one defined problem. A student who knows the material should be able to answer without reading the options. For scenario-based items, include only the context essential to the problem. Avoid negative stems; if used, capitalize NOT or EXCEPT.
Cover the options and read only the stem. If the question is unclear without the choices, rewrite it.
3
Construct Options
Provide 4 options: one correct answer and 3 plausible distractors. Build distractors from common student errors with maritime regulations, procedures, or calculations. Ensure grammatical and structural consistency across all options. Arrange in logical order.
After drafting, scan for repeated opening words across options — consolidate them into the stem to reduce reading load.
4
Review Content Validity
Confirm the correct answer is the single, clearly defensible best response under current maritime regulations (SOLAS, STCW, MARPOL, ColRegs). Distractors should reflect partial truths or common procedural misconceptions. Avoid "all/none of the above" and absolute terms.
Ask a colleague to identify the correct answer without context. If they hesitate between two options, revise.
5
Peer Review
Have another maritime instructor review for ambiguity, fairness, regulatory accuracy, and alignment with the learning outcome. Verify that no surface feature unintentionally signals the answer.
6
Record and Validate
Record in the Test Item Bank with: learning outcome, answer key, Bloom's level, applicable maritime regulation or standard (e.g., STCW Reg. II/1), and distractor rationale for post-test analysis.

Stem Design — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR STEM
Which of these does NOT indicate that a vessel does not need to sound a fog signal?
a) Vessel is underway with engines
b) Visibility exceeds 5 nautical miles
c) Vessel is at anchor with a bell
d) Vessel is constrained by draft
Issues: Double negative, unclear intent, options needed to understand the question, filler phrasing.
✓ IMPROVED STEM
Under ColRegs Rule 35, a power-driven vessel underway in restricted visibility must sound a prolonged blast at intervals of not more than:
a) 1 minute
b) 2 minutes
c) 3 minutes
d) 5 minutes
Positive framing, full context in stem, regulation cited, direct question, one clear answer (b).

Distractor Design — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR DISTRACTORS
What governs the minimum safe manning of a vessel?
a) The captain's personal decision
b) Any international agreement
c) Some kind of maritime law
d) A document from international bodies
Issues: Vague, overlapping distractors (b, c, d describe the same thing broadly), far-fetched option (a).
✓ IMPROVED DISTRACTORS
The minimum safe manning of a vessel is primarily determined by:
a) The shipowner's discretion
b) The port state control authority
c) The Safe Manning Certificate issued by the flag state
d) The crew's collective bargaining agreement
Each option is distinct, plausible, and reflects a real misconception. One clear answer (c). Mutually exclusive.

Correct AnswerSingle defensible best answer per current regulationsTwo arguably correct options
DistractorsPlausible; reflect real procedural or regulatory misconceptionsFar-fetched or obviously wrong options

Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓ Align every item to a specific maritime learning outcome
✓ State the full scenario or problem in the stem
✓ Use direct questions, not incomplete sentences
✓ Build distractors from real student misconceptions about regulations
✓ Verify regulatory accuracy (SOLAS, STCW, ColRegs, MARPOL) before finalizing
✓ Keep all options grammatically consistent with the stem
✓ Aim for 4 options: 1 correct + 3 plausible distractors
✓ Randomly distribute the correct answer across positions
✗ DON'T
✗ Use negative or double-negative stems
✗ Include "All of the above" or "None of the above"
✗ Open stems with "Which of the following…" filler
✗ Allow two options that are arguably both correct
✗ Include absolute terms: always, never, all, none, only
✗ Make the correct answer noticeably longer than distractors
✗ Use outdated regulation references without reviewing current standards
✗ Take test bank items without checking alignment and regulatory accuracy
2
Long Structured Essay
Assess extended analysis, synthesis, evaluation, and multi-part argumentation
Higher-Order ThinkingMulti-Part PromptsRubric Design
▼
A Long Structured Essay requires students to produce an extended, organized, multi-paragraph response to a complex maritime prompt. It is the primary tool for assessing whether students can integrate knowledge across a topic — analyzing the causes and consequences of a maritime incident, evaluating crew emergency response, or proposing a complete safety management plan. The "structured" element means the prompt provides clear numbered sub-questions, making responses more targeted and scoring more consistent.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define Purpose and Cognitive Level
Identify which higher-order competence the question will measure: analysis, synthesis, or evaluation. A Long Structured Essay should always target at least Analysis level on Bloom's Taxonomy.
Ask: "Do I want students to describe a procedure, or to analyze why it succeeded/failed, or to design a better one?" The answer determines the directive verb.
2
Write a Structured Prompt with Sub-Questions
Open with a maritime scenario (e.g., a machinery breakdown, grounding incident, or MARPOL violation). Follow with 2–4 numbered sub-questions using specific directive verbs: Analyze, Evaluate, Compare, Justify, Recommend, Propose.
Each sub-question should be answerable in 1–3 focused paragraphs. If one requires more, split it further.
3
Clarify Parameters for Each Part
State the expected total length (e.g., 500–700 words), time allocation (e.g., 45 minutes), and mark breakdown per sub-question. Showing the mark allocation guides students on the expected depth of each part.
Allocate marks per sub-question and show the breakdown in the prompt itself — e.g., "Question 1 (10 pts)."
4
Develop a Structured Scoring Rubric
Create a rubric with separate criteria for each sub-question covering: technical accuracy, depth of analysis, quality of reasoning and evidence, logical organization, and clarity of expression.
Write a model answer before finalizing the rubric. If you struggle to describe excellent performance, the sub-question is too vague.
5
Review for Clarity, Fairness, and Technical Accuracy
Verify all scenario details against current maritime regulations and standard procedures (SOLAS, STCW, ISM Code). Have a subject matter expert attempt the prompt before issuing it.
6
Validate and Record
Include the approved version in the Assessment Instrument Register with: the full rubric, mark allocation per sub-question, learning outcome mapped, cognitive level tagged, applicable maritime standard, and estimated completion time.

Prompt — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR PROMPT
Discuss the MV Sewol disaster.
(No sub-questions, no directive verbs, no scope, no parameters)
Issues: "Discuss" is undefined. No cognitive task specified. Students may write a narrative summary rather than analysis.
✓ IMPROVED PROMPT
A passenger ro-ro ferry overloads cargo beyond its stability limits and capsizes in poor weather with significant loss of life.
1. (10 pts) Analyze three seamanship and stability factors that contributed to the capsizing.
2. (10 pts) Evaluate the adequacy of the crew's emergency response.
3. (10 pts) Recommend amendments to loading procedures to prevent recurrence.
(500–700 words · 45 minutes)
Structured sub-questions, specific directive verbs, mark allocation shown, length and time stated.

Design Checklist

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid
ScenarioRealistic maritime context with sufficient technical detailVague scenario that allows any interpretation
Sub-Questions2–4 parts, each with a distinct directive verb and cognitive taskOne broad question mixing multiple tasks
Directive VerbsAnalyze, Evaluate, Justify, Recommend, Compare, ProposeVague verbs: "discuss," "explain," "describe"
ParametersLength, time, and mark allocation stated explicitly per sub-questionNo guidance on expected depth, length, or marks
RubricSeparate criteria per sub-question; written before finalizing promptSingle holistic rubric for the entire response
Technical AccuracyAll scenario details verified against current maritime conventionsOutdated or inaccurate technical details in the scenario

Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓ Use a realistic maritime scenario as the prompt context
✓ Include 2–4 numbered sub-questions with distinct directive verbs
✓ Show the mark allocation per sub-question in the prompt
✓ State expected total length and time allocation
✓ Develop a separate rubric for each sub-question
✓ Verify all technical details against SOLAS, STCW, MARPOL, ISM Code
✓ Have a maritime expert attempt the prompt before issuing it
✗ DON'T
✗ Use a single open question like "Discuss X" without structure
✗ Omit sub-questions, leaving students to determine scope
✗ Leave length, time, or mark allocation unspecified
✗ Use vague verbs like "discuss," "write about," or "explain"
✗ Finalize the prompt before writing the rubric
✗ Score responses without a rubric (impressionistic marking)
✗ Omit the rubric from the Assessment Instrument Register
3
Essay — Short Answer (One Paragraph)
Assess focused understanding, explanation, or application in a single coherent paragraph
Focused ResponseSingle ParagraphDirective Verbs
▼
An Essay question at this level requires a single, focused paragraph — typically 5–10 sentences — in response to a specific prompt. It sits between a fill-in-the-blank item and a full long structured essay: more cognitively demanding than recall-based items, but more constrained than multi-part argumentation. In maritime education, these questions are ideal for testing whether a student can explain the purpose of a regulation, describe a standard procedure, or briefly apply a principle to a given on-board situation.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define the Cognitive Demand
Identify whether the item targets comprehension (explain, describe) or application (apply a regulation to a situation). Essay questions at this level should go beyond pure recall — the student should construct a response in their own words, not reproduce memorized text.
Ask: "Would a student who truly understands this topic be able to write this paragraph without memorizing it word-for-word?" If yes, it's a good essay question.
2
Write the Prompt with a Precise Directive Verb
Use a single, specific directive verb: Explain, Describe, State and explain, Briefly discuss, Outline. The prompt should specify exactly what to address.
Avoid "Write a paragraph about X." Instead: "Explain the purpose of the ISM Code and identify one specific obligation it places on the Master."
3
Specify the Expected Format
State clearly: one paragraph, 5–8 sentences, or approximately 80–120 words. Without a format constraint, students either write a single sentence (too brief) or begin writing an essay (too long).
4
Develop an Answer Key with Key Points
Write a model paragraph before finalizing. From this, extract 3–5 key content points that a complete response should include. Assign 1–2 marks per key point.
If your model answer requires more than one paragraph, either reduce the scope or reclassify as a Long Structured Essay.
5
Review for Clarity and Scope
Ensure the prompt cannot be answered in one sentence and would not naturally require multiple paragraphs. A well-calibrated essay prompt has a clearly bounded scope answerable in one focused paragraph.
6
Record and Archive
Store in the Item Bank with: the model answer, key content points and marks per point, learning outcome, cognitive level tagged, and applicable maritime standard.

Prompt — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR PROMPT
Write about MARPOL.
(No directive verb, no scope, no format guidance)
Issues: Scope is the entire MARPOL convention. Students cannot know whether to describe, analyze, or evaluate, or which Annex to focus on.
✓ IMPROVED PROMPT
Explain the purpose of MARPOL Annex I and briefly describe two specific discharge restrictions it places on oil tankers operating in Special Areas.
(One paragraph · 5–8 sentences · 10 minutes)
Specific directive verbs (Explain, describe), bounded scope (Annex I, Special Areas, two restrictions), format and time stated.

Design Checklist

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid
Directive VerbExplain, Describe, Outline, State and explainVague: "write about," "discuss," "talk about"
ScopeClearly bounded — answerable in one focused paragraphToo broad (needs multiple paragraphs) or too narrow (one sentence)
FormatState "one paragraph" and approximate length or sentence countNo format guidance given to students
Answer Key3–5 key content points; model paragraph written before finalizingVague key applied differently by different markers
DepthTargets comprehension or application — not pure recallQuestion answerable by reproducing a memorized definition
AlignmentMaps to a specific maritime learning outcome or standardItems testing content not meaningfully taught

Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓ Use specific directive verbs (Explain, Describe, Outline)
✓ Bound the scope so a one-paragraph answer is sufficient
✓ State "one paragraph" and expected sentence count or word limit
✓ Write the model paragraph before finalizing the question
✓ Extract 3–5 key content points as scoring criteria
✓ Target comprehension or application, not rote memorization
✗ DON'T
✗ Use "Write about X" or "Discuss X" without specifying the task
✗ Set scope so broad it naturally requires multiple paragraphs
✗ Set scope so narrow it can be answered in one or two sentences
✗ Leave format (length, paragraph count) unspecified
✗ Finalize without writing the model answer first
✗ Score impressionistically without a key-point scoring guide
4
Fill-in-the-Blank / Identification / Completion
Assess precise recall of specific terms, names, regulation references, and component identifications
Exact RecallTerminologyIdentification
▼
This item type family covers three closely related formats. Fill-in-the-Blank presents a statement with a gap to complete. Identification prompts for a specific term, name, component, or regulation. Completion presents an incomplete definition, formula, or procedural statement to finish. In maritime education, this format is ideal for testing equipment names, regulation numbers, code provisions, signal meanings, and component functions.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Identify Target Knowledge
Select facts, terms, equipment names, regulation titles, or values that are central to the learning outcome and worth recalling precisely.
Ask: "Is precise recall of this exact term or value a meaningful indicator of maritime competence?" If not, choose a different item type.
2
Choose the Appropriate Sub-Format
Fill-in-the-Blank: Complete sentence with one blank at or near the end. Best for terms within a context.
Identification: Direct prompt using "Identify…" or "Name…". Best for specific names, regulation references, or component IDs.
Completion: Incomplete definition, formula, or procedural statement. Best for precise technical definitions or sequences.
For regulation numbers and proper nouns, the "Identify…" format is often clearer than a blank in a sentence.
3
Avoid Grammatical Giveaways
Articles (a vs. an), verb agreement, and plural forms can unintentionally signal the correct answer. Replace "a/an" immediately before a blank with "a/an (as appropriate)" or restructure the sentence.
4
Define the Expected Answer Precisely
Before finalizing, write every acceptable correct answer — including official abbreviations, alternate names (e.g., "GMDSS" and "Global Maritime Distress and Safety System"). If the list becomes too long, the item is too ambiguous.
For regulation references, decide in advance whether you accept short form (e.g., "SOLAS II-1") or require full titles, and state this in the instructions.
5
Review Readability and Add Cues
Ensure the remaining context clearly indicates what type of answer is expected. Add a brief cue in parentheses if needed: "(equipment name)," "(SOLAS regulation number)," "(in nautical miles)."
6
Record in Item Bank
Store with: the full answer key (all acceptable forms and abbreviations), learning outcome, competence type tagged as "Knowledge-based," and the applicable maritime standard being tested.

Examples — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR ITEMS
Fill-in: "The __________ is used when a ship is in distress."
Identification: "What is the equipment for emergencies?"
Completion: "MARPOL is a convention that..."
Issues: Blank at start; too vague for one answer; open-ended with many possible completions.
✓ IMPROVED ITEMS
Fill-in: "When a vessel is in distress, the OOW should activate the __________ (equipment name) to alert rescue coordination centers automatically."
Identification: "Identify the SOLAS regulation requiring muster drills within 24 hours of departure from port. (Regulation number)"
Completion: "MARPOL Annex VI sets limits on __________ (two words) emissions from ship exhausts."
Answers: EPIRB; Reg. III/19; air pollution.

Design Checklist

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid
PositionBlank at or near end; identification uses "Identify/Name" verbBlank at the beginning of the sentence
Answer CueAdd parenthetical cue when answer type is not obviousNo indication of whether answer is a name, number, or regulation
GrammarNeutral articles; no grammatical clues to singular/plurala/an or verb form revealing the answer type
Answer KeyAll acceptable forms listed before finalizingAccepting too many variant answers (item too vague)
ContextRemaining text clearly points to the specific missing informationStatement that works for multiple different correct answers
SpecificityTests central, precisely-defined maritime knowledgeTesting trivial details or information not taught in class

Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓ Place blanks at or near the end of sentences
✓ Use "Identify…" or "Name…" for proper nouns and regulation references
✓ Add a parenthetical cue when the answer type is not obvious
✓ List all acceptable answer forms (abbreviations, full titles, alternate names)
✓ Test central maritime knowledge worth knowing precisely
✓ Review grammar to remove unintentional answer clues
✗ DON'T
✗ Place blanks at the beginning of sentences
✗ Include two or more blanks in a single sentence
✗ Use a/an immediately before a blank
✗ Write items where three or more substantially different answers are all acceptable
✗ Test obscure details barely mentioned in instruction
✗ Allow grammar or structure to narrow the answer type
5
True or False
Assess essential factual knowledge of maritime regulations, standards, and procedures
Single-Idea FocusAbsolute AccuracyRegulatory Currency
▼
True/False items are the most efficient format for assessing large volumes of factual maritime content quickly — ideal for checking knowledge of specific regulation requirements, signal meanings, equipment specifications, and procedural facts. Their primary weakness is a 50% guessing probability, so they must be used in clusters of 10 or more. In maritime education, where regulations have precise technical thresholds, the requirement for a statement to be 100% true or 100% false is especially critical.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Identify the Learning Point
Focus on a single, important fact, regulation requirement, or technical specification. Reserve True/False items for clear-cut, well-defined maritime knowledge. Avoid nuanced topics where context or vessel type changes the answer.
Ask: "Is this statement unambiguously true or false under current regulations, regardless of context?" If the answer depends on vessel type or area, choose MCQ instead.
2
Construct the Statement
Write a single, concise, factual sentence in plain language. Each statement must contain exactly one idea — never combine two separate regulatory requirements.
Read the statement to a colleague. If they say "it depends" or ask a clarifying question, revise the statement.
3
Ensure the Statement Is 100% True or 100% False
A statement that is "true for most vessels" is not acceptable. If exceptions exist, either specify the scope precisely in the statement or choose a different item type.
Have a maritime expert review. If they add "yes, but only for vessels over 500 GT…" — the statement is not universally true and must be revised.
4
Avoid Negatives and Absolute Qualifiers
Words like always, never, all, none, and only allow students to guess False regardless of content knowledge. Write direct, affirmative factual statements.
5
Verify Regulatory Accuracy and Currency
Verify all statements against current versions of applicable maritime instruments (SOLAS, STCW, MARPOL, ColRegs, ISM Code, MLC). Record the specific regulation reference and amendment year in item metadata.
6
Validate, Balance, and Record
Peer review for factual accuracy. Use true and false items in roughly equal proportions. Record in the Item Bank with answer key, regulation reference, and applicable maritime instrument and amendment year.
After drafting a full set, count True and False answers. If the split exceeds 60/40 in either direction, revise for better balance.

Statement — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR STATEMENT
All ships must always carry a copy of the ISM Code on board and never operate without one.
True  /  False
Issues: Combines two claims, uses absolute qualifiers ("all," "always," "never"), and the ISM Code applies only to vessels above a certain size.
✓ IMPROVED STATEMENT
Under the ISM Code, vessels of 500 GT and above engaged on international voyages are required to hold a valid Safety Management Certificate (SMC).
True  /  False   (Answer: True)
Single idea, precise scope specified (500 GT, international voyages), factually accurate, no absolute qualifiers.

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid
Single IdeaOne regulation, fact, or specification per statementCombining two separate regulatory requirements
Accuracy100% true or false; no exceptions within stated scopeStatements true for some vessel types but not others without scope
LanguagePlain, direct, affirmative; no negativesDouble negatives or "not" constructions
QualifiersAvoid always/never/all/none/onlyAbsolute terms that signal False regardless of content
CurrencyVerified against current convention/amendment yearUsing outdated or superseded regulation references
BalanceRoughly equal true and false items across the setHeavy imbalance enabling pattern-guessing

Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓ Focus each statement on one single regulatory fact or specification
✓ Specify scope (vessel type, tonnage, voyage type) when necessary
✓ Verify accuracy against the current version of the applicable convention
✓ Use plain, direct, affirmative language
✓ Balance true and false items roughly equally across the set
✓ Use in clusters of 10+ for adequate reliability
✗ DON'T
✗ Combine two separate regulatory requirements in one statement
✗ Write statements true for some vessels but not others without specifying scope
✗ Use absolute terms: always, never, all, none, only
✗ Use outdated regulation references
✗ Allow heavy imbalance of true vs. false items
✗ Rely on True/False alone for high-stakes summative assessments
6
Matching
Assess ability to link maritime terms, regulations, components, and definitions
Relationship TestingHomogeneous ListsLayout Design
▼
Matching items efficiently test whether students can identify correct relationships between two sets of related maritime content — such as SOLAS chapters and their scope, types of fire and appropriate extinguishing agents, navigation lights and their arc of visibility, or MARPOL Annexes and the type of pollution they regulate. They reduce guessing compared to True/False and cover multiple related concepts efficiently. Key challenges: keeping both columns homogeneous, ensuring correct matches are unambiguous, and adding dummy responses to prevent elimination guessing.

Step-by-Step Process

1
Define the Relationship Type
Clearly identify what type of relationship students will match: regulation ↔ scope, equipment ↔ function, signal ↔ meaning, MARPOL Annex ↔ pollutant type, fire class ↔ extinguishing agent. The relationship type must be consistent across all pairs.
State the relationship explicitly in the instructions: "Match each MARPOL Annex in Column A with the type of pollution it addresses in Column B."
2
Prepare Homogeneous, Parallel Lists
Develop 6–10 pairs. All items in Column A must belong to the same category, and all items in Column B must belong to the same category. Mixing categories makes correct matches obvious without content knowledge.
Read all Column A items together — they should feel like members of the same set. Do the same for Column B.
3
Add Dummy Responses
Include 1–2 extra responses in Column B that have no correct match in Column A. This prevents process-of-elimination guessing for the last answer. Dummy responses must be as plausible as the correct responses.
4
Write Clear, Unambiguous Instructions
State explicitly: (a) the relationship being matched, (b) whether each response can be used more than once or only once, and (c) how to record the answer.
5
Organize the Layout Thoughtfully
Place longer text (definitions, descriptions) in Column A (left) and shorter text (terms, codes, Annex numbers) in Column B (right). Arrange Column B responses in logical order — alphabetical, numerical, or by Annex/chapter number.
6
Review for Plausibility and Validate
Ensure no unintended surface clues give away any match. All responses should be plausible for at least some stems.
Cover Column A and look only at Column B. If any response is clearly unrelated to the others, it's not a plausible dummy — revise it.

Example — Poor vs. Improved

✗ POOR SET
Column A: 1. SOLAS   2. Chief Engineer   3. Red flare   4. Knot
Column B: A. Unit of speed   B. Safety of life at sea   C. Distress signal   D. Senior engineer officer
Issues: Mixed categories in Column A (convention, job title, signal type, unit of measure). Correct matches are obvious without content knowledge.
✓ IMPROVED SET
Column A (MARPOL Annexes): I. Annex I   II. Annex II   III. Annex IV   IV. Annex V   V. Annex VI
Column B (Pollution Type): A. Garbage   B. Air emissions   C. Sewage   D. Oil   E. Noxious liquids   F. ★ Ballast water (dummy)
Homogeneous, logical numerical order, one plausible dummy (F). Answers: I-D, II-E, III-C, IV-A, V-B.

Design Checklist

ElementKey PrincipleCommon Pitfall to Avoid
RelationshipOne consistent relationship type throughout the setMixing different relationship types in one set
HomogeneityAll Column A items same category; all Column B items same categoryMixed categories making correct matches obvious
Dummy Responses1–2 plausible extras in Column B from the same categoryNo extras — enables process-of-elimination guessing
InstructionsState relationship, reuse rules, and response format explicitlyAmbiguous instructions about reuse of responses

PlausibilityNo surface clues (terminology, grammar) link a stem to its responseRepeated key terms in both columns giving away matches

Do's and Don'ts

✓ DO
✓ Use one consistent relationship type throughout the set
✓ Keep all items in each column from the same category
✓ Add 1–2 plausible dummy responses from the same category to Column B
✓ State clearly whether responses can be used more than once
✓ Place longer descriptions in Column A, shorter terms/codes in Column B
✓ Validate and submit to Item Bank with full answer key and regulation cited
✗ DON'T
✗ Mix different categories within Column A or Column B
✗ Omit dummy responses from Column B
✗ Leave instructions ambiguous about reuse of responses
✗ Repeat key terms from Column A in Column B responses
✗ Use fewer than 6 pairs (too susceptible to elimination)
✗ Use outdated regulation, code, or convention names
II

Guidelines for Selecting Assessment Item Types

Choosing the right item type is as important as writing the item well — guided by cognitive level, content nature, logistics, and purpose

✦
Key Factors in Selecting Item Types
Seven factors to guide your assessment design decisions
▼
1
Learning Objective / Competency Level (Bloom's Taxonomy)
Knowledge / Recall — True/False, Fill-in-the-Blank, Identification, Matching
Comprehension / Understanding — MCQ, Essay (1-paragraph)
Application — Scenario-based MCQ, Essay (1-paragraph)
Analysis / Evaluation / Synthesis — Long Structured Essay, Case Studies
2
Nature of the Content
Regulation references, component names, signal codes — Identification, Fill-in-the-Blank, Matching
Conceptual understanding of procedures or standards — MCQ, Essay (1-paragraph)
Regulatory relationships (Annex ↔ pollutant, chapter ↔ scope) — Matching
Complex incident analysis or emergency decision-making — Long Structured Essay
3
Number of Examinees / Scoring Efficiency
Large cohorts — MCQ, True/False, Matching for efficient, consistent scoring
Smaller classes or competency assessments — Essay types provide richer evidence of competence
4
Time Constraints
Limited time — Objective items cover more content per minute (MCQ, T/F, Matching)
Sufficient time — Long Structured Essay enables authentic demonstration of maritime competence
5
Reliability vs. Validity Trade-off
Objective items — Higher reliability (consistent scoring across markers), but may not capture complex maritime decision-making
Subjective items — Higher validity for measuring real officer competence, but require rubrics to achieve acceptable reliability
6
Purpose of Assessment
Formative (ongoing checks) — Essay (1-paragraph), T/F, Fill-in-the-Blank
Summative (end-of-course, pre-licensure) — Mixed sets: MCQ + Long Structured Essay + Identification
Diagnostic — MCQ with targeted distractors, Fill-in-the-Blank, True/False
7
Mixing Item Types (Test Blueprints)
Cover different cognitive levels across Bloom's Taxonomy — from recall to evaluation
Sample a broad range of maritime competencies efficiently within available time
Improve content validity by representing multiple STCW/SOLAS competency areas
Balance objective scoring efficiency with depth of demonstrated maritime competence
